Suppose we want to minimise an objective $f(x)$ while also staying close to a
specific point $y$. There are two natural ways to encode "stay close to $y$":

1. **Add a penalty term.** Minimise $f(x) + \lambda D(x, y)$ to convergence. The
   penalty $\lambda$ trades off the objective against the distance to $y$.

   $$
   x_\lambda = \arg\min_x \; f(x) + \frac{\lambda}{2}\, \| x - y \|^2
   $$

2. **Start at $y$ and stop early.** Run gradient descent on $f$ alone, but
   *initialise* at $x_0 = y$ and take only $T$ steps. You never get far from $y$
   simply because you don't have the budget to.

   $$
   x_T = \text{GD}_T(f;\, x_0 = y), \qquad x_{t+1} = x_t - \eta\, \nabla f(x_t)
   $$

Is there a rigorous connection between them? **Yes — but it is an approximation,
not an equivalence**, and the difference matters. This post derives the exact
relationship for a quadratic objective, where both methods turn out to be
*spectral filters*, and shows precisely when they agree and when they don't.

## The setup: a quadratic objective

Take

$$
f(x) = \tfrac{1}{2}(x - x_\star)^\top H (x - x_\star), \qquad H \succ 0,
$$

with minimiser $x_\star$ and Hessian $H$ having eigenpairs $(h_i, v_i)$. Let

$$
g \;=\; \nabla f(y) \;=\; H(y - x_\star)
$$

be the gradient at our anchor point $y$. Everything below decouples across the
eigendirections of $H$, so it is enough to track each component $g_i = v_i^\top g$
separately. Note that the **full step to the optimum** along direction $i$ is

$$
(x_\star - y)_i = -\,\frac{g_i}{h_i},
$$

so any method that lands at $y - c_i \frac{g_i}{h_i}$ has moved a fraction $c_i \in
[0,1]$ of the way to $x_\star$ in that direction. Both methods below have exactly
this form; they differ only in the fractions $c_i$.

## Method 1: the penalty solution

Stationarity of $f(x) + \tfrac{\lambda}{2}\|x-y\|^2$ gives

$$
H(x - x_\star) + \lambda (x - y) = 0
\;\;\Longrightarrow\;\;
(H + \lambda I)\,x = H x_\star + \lambda y.
$$

Subtract $(H + \lambda I)\,y$ from both sides and use $H(x_\star - y) = -g$:

$$
(H + \lambda I)(x_\lambda - y) = H(x_\star - y) = -g
\;\;\Longrightarrow\;\;
x_\lambda - y = -(H + \lambda I)^{-1} g.
$$

Per eigendirection,

$$
(x_\lambda - y)_i \;=\; -\,\frac{g_i}{h_i + \lambda}
\;=\; -\,\frac{g_i}{h_i}\,\underbrace{\frac{h_i}{h_i + \lambda}}_{\text{ridge filter } r_\lambda(h_i)}.
$$

So ridge moves a fraction $r_\lambda(h) = \frac{h}{h+\lambda} \in (0,1)$ of the way
to the optimum in each direction. High-curvature directions ($h \gg \lambda$) are
followed almost all the way; low-curvature directions ($h \ll \lambda$) are barely
moved. This is the familiar shrinkage behaviour of ridge regression.

## Method 2: the early-stopping solution

Gradient descent on $f$ alone is $x_{t+1} = x_t - \eta H(x_t - x_\star)$. Writing
$e_t = x_t - x_\star$, this is $e_{t+1} = (I - \eta H)\,e_t$, so after $T$ steps
$e_T = (I - \eta H)^T e_0$ with $e_0 = y - x_\star$. Therefore

$$
x_T - y = \big[(I - \eta H)^T - I\big](y - x_\star)
\;=\; -\big[I - (I - \eta H)^T\big] H^{-1} g.
$$

Per eigendirection,

$$
(x_T - y)_i \;=\; -\,\frac{g_i}{h_i}\,\underbrace{\big[1 - (1 - \eta h_i)^T\big]}_{\text{GD filter } \phi_{\eta,T}(h_i)}.
$$

Same form as ridge — a fraction of the full step — but with a *different* filter
$\phi_{\eta,T}(h) = 1 - (1 - \eta h)^T$. For a small step ($\eta h \ll 1$) we have
$(1 - \eta h)^T \approx e^{-\eta h T}$, so

$$
\phi_{\eta,T}(h) \;\approx\; 1 - e^{-\eta T h},
$$

which is exactly the **gradient-flow** filter after continuous time $t = \eta T$.

## Where the connection lives — and where it breaks

Now compare the two filters. Define the dimensionless **effective progress**
$s = \eta T h$ and set the penalty to

$$
\boxed{\;\lambda = \frac{1}{\eta T}\;}
$$

Then the ridge filter becomes

$$
r_\lambda(h) = \frac{h}{h + 1/(\eta T)} = \frac{\eta T h}{1 + \eta T h} = \frac{s}{1+s},
$$

while the early-stopping filter is $\phi \approx 1 - e^{-s}$. Taylor-expanding both:

$$
1 - e^{-s} = s - \tfrac{1}{2}s^2 + \cdots,
\qquad
\frac{s}{1+s} = s - s^2 + \cdots.
$$

**They agree to first order in $s$, and disagree at second order.** This is the
real content of the "connection": $\lambda = 1/(\eta T)$ is the correct
correspondence, but it is only accurate where $s = \eta T h$ is small — i.e. the
slow, low-curvature directions that neither method has moved much. For the
high-curvature directions, $s \gtrsim 1$ and the filters part ways:

| $s = \eta T h$ | early stopping $1 - e^{-s}$ | ridge $\frac{s}{1+s}$ |
|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| 0.2 | 0.181 | 0.167 |
| 1.0 | 0.632 | 0.500 |
| 2.0 | 0.865 | 0.667 |
| 6.0 | 0.998 | 0.857 |

Early stopping rushes the high-curvature directions to convergence
(*exponentially* in $s$); ridge only ever gets there as $1 - 1/s$. By $s=2$ they
disagree by 20 percentage points.

### Why no single $\lambda$ can fix this

You might hope a better choice of $\lambda$ would match GD exactly. It can't —
not with a single scalar. Matching the filters in direction $i$ requires

$$
\frac{h_i}{h_i + \lambda} = 1 - (1 - \eta h_i)^T
\;\;\Longrightarrow\;\;
\lambda_i = h_i\,\frac{(1 - \eta h_i)^T}{1 - (1 - \eta h_i)^T}.
$$

This $\lambda_i$ **depends on the curvature** $h_i$: it tends to $1/(\eta T)$ for
the slow directions and falls toward $0$ for the already-converged fast ones. A
single penalty $\lambda$ applies one shrinkage profile across the whole spectrum;
early stopping applies a different one. They are genuinely different
regularisation paths. $\lambda = 1/(\eta T)$ is just the best single compromise —
exact for the slow directions, optimistic for the fast ones.

## Numerical check

A 50-dimensional random quadratic, $\eta = 0.01$, $T = 200$, so $\lambda =
1/(\eta T) = 0.5$:

```python
import numpy as np
np.random.seed(0)
d = 50
Q, _ = np.linalg.qr(np.random.randn(d, d))
h = np.sort(np.random.uniform(0.1, 10.0, d))   # eigenvalues of H
H = Q @ np.diag(h) @ Q.T
xstar, y = np.random.randn(d), np.random.randn(d)

def gd(eta, T):
    x = y.copy()
    for _ in range(T):
        x -= eta * (H @ (x - xstar))
    return x

def ridge(lam):
    return np.linalg.solve(H + lam*np.eye(d), H @ xstar + lam*y)

eta, T = 0.01, 200
xgd, xr = gd(eta, T), ridge(1/(eta*T))
print(np.linalg.norm(xgd - xr) / np.linalg.norm(xgd - y))   # ~0.10
```

The two solutions sit about **10% apart** (relative to how far each moved from
$y$) — small enough to see the connection, large enough that calling them
"equivalent" is wrong. Shrink the spectrum or take fewer steps (push every $s$
toward $0$) and the gap closes; widen it and the gap grows.

## Takeaways

- Starting at $y$ and stopping early **is** a form of regularisation towards $y$,
  and the matching penalty is $\lambda \approx 1/(\eta T)$. More steps (or a bigger
  step size) means weaker regularisation — exactly as intuition suggests.
- But it is **not** the same as an $\ell_2$ penalty. Both are spectral filters of
  the curvature; ridge uses $\frac{h}{h+\lambda}$, early stopping uses
  $1 - (1-\eta h)^T$. They match only to first order in $s = \eta T h$, so they
  agree on slow directions and diverge on fast ones.
- The honest small parameter is $s = \eta T h$, not $\eta$. Any claim of
  equivalence has to say *in which directions* and *to what order*.

This is a known result. The continuous-time picture (gradient flow $\leftrightarrow$
ridge with $\lambda = 1/t$, plus tight risk bounds) is worked out by Ali, Kolter &
Tibshirani, *A Continuous-Time View of Early Stopping for Least Squares*
(AISTATS 2019); the statistical learning version is Yao, Rosasco & Caponnetto,
*On Early Stopping in Gradient Descent Learning* (2007); and the unifying
"both are spectral filters" view comes from the spectral-algorithms / Landweber-
iteration literature on inverse problems.
