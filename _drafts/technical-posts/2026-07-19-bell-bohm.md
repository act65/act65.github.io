*A caution before I start: I am not a physicist, this is contested territory even among the experts, and I've tried to flag where the careful people genuinely disagree. But there's one thing in here that is not a matter of taste — a popular story that is simply false — and it's worth getting straight, because I keep meeting people who have quietly given up on the pilot-wave picture for the wrong reason.*

If you find the de Broglie–Bohm pilot-wave interpretation of quantum mechanics appealing — particles with definite positions at all times, guided by the wave function, no observer-triggered collapse, no dead-and-alive cats — you have probably been told, at some point, that Bell's theorem killed it. That Bell proved hidden variables are impossible, and Bohm's theory is a hidden-variable theory, so that's the end of it.

This is wrong. Not "controversial" wrong — historically-backwards wrong. Bell's theorem does not rule out Bohmian mechanics. Bohmian mechanics was the *inspiration* for Bell's theorem, and Bell spent his career as pilot-wave theory's most vocal defender. Let me lay out why, as carefully as I can.

### What Bell's theorem actually rules out

The slogan "Bell ruled out hidden variables" drops the crucial word. What Bell ruled out is **local** hidden-variable theories — or, stated more carefully, any theory that satisfies two assumptions at once:

1. **Locality** (Bell locality / local causality): once you know the complete physical state of a pair of particles, the outcome on one side does not depend on the *setting* or *outcome* chosen at the distant side. No spooky influence across the gap.
2. **Statistical independence** ("free choice" / no superdeterminism): the settings the experimenters choose are independent of the hidden state of the particles — the experiment isn't rigged in advance by a common cause.

Grant both, and Bell derives an inequality (the CHSH inequality) that any such theory must obey. Quantum mechanics predicts a violation of that inequality, and — this is the part that took decades to nail down experimentally — nature actually violates it. So *no* theory can be both local and statistically-independent and match reality. One of those assumptions has to go.

> There's a genuine framing dispute here that I have to flag, because careful people fight about it. The textbook and popular-press version says the assumptions are "locality **and realism**," and that you can rescue locality by abandoning "realism" (roughly, the idea that measurements reveal pre-existing values). But Bell himself, and philosophers like Tim Maudlin and the Stanford Encyclopedia's treatment, argue this is misleading: "realism" isn't a separate premise you get to drop — the pre-existing values are *derived* (via the EPR argument) from locality plus the observed perfect correlations, and then shown impossible. On this reading, the honest conclusion of Bell's theorem is stark: **nature is nonlocal.** Full stop. Not "nature is nonlocal *or* non-realist" — nonlocal. I'll follow Bell's own reading here, while noting the other framing is widespread.

### Where Bohm sits

Now place Bohmian mechanics against those two assumptions. It keeps a wave function evolving by the Schrödinger equation exactly as usual, and it adds **actual particle positions** — the "hidden variables" — that ride along, steered by a guiding equation. It is fully deterministic, and given the natural starting distribution ($|\psi|^2$, "quantum equilibrium") it reproduces *every* prediction of standard non-relativistic quantum mechanics. Not an approximation; the same numbers.

And it is **explicitly, unashamedly nonlocal.** The velocity of one particle depends, instantaneously, on the positions of every other particle it's entangled with, however far away — because the guiding wave lives in the configuration space of the whole system, not in ordinary 3D space. Bohm *keeps* statistical independence (experimenters choose freely) and *sacrifices locality*. Technically, it violates what's called Parameter Independence: the outcome here can depend on the setting there.

So Bohmian mechanics does not sneak past Bell's theorem through a loophole. It walks straight through the front door and accepts the conclusion. Bell's theorem says any theory matching reality must be nonlocal; Bohm *is* nonlocal; therefore Bohm is exactly the kind of theory Bell's theorem says you're allowed to have. It is **consistent with Bell's theorem** in the strongest sense — it's a worked example of what surviving the theorem looks like.

### The reframe that should settle your nerves

Here's the thing that I think dissolves the worry entirely. Nonlocality is not Bohm's embarrassing secret that the other interpretations avoid. It's **everyone's**. Bell's later analysis shows the nonlocality is a feature of the quantum *predictions themselves* — any single-world account of quantum phenomena has to be nonlocal, orthodox quantum mechanics included. Ordinary QM just *hides* its nonlocality inside the vagueness of "measurement" and "collapse." As Bell put it, "it is a merit of the de Broglie–Bohm version to bring this out so explicitly that it cannot be ignored." Bohm isn't paying a price the others escape; Bohm is being honest about a bill everyone owes.

### The history, which is the tell

If Bell's theorem refuted Bohm, you'd expect Bell to have been anti-Bohm. The opposite is true, and vividly so.

In 1932 von Neumann had published a famous "proof" that no hidden-variable theory could reproduce quantum mechanics. For twenty years it was taken as settled. Then, in 1952, Bohm built a hidden-variable theory that did exactly the "impossible" thing. Bell's own words: *"in 1952 I saw the impossible done. It was in papers by David Bohm."* Bell went back and found the flaw in von Neumann's proof (it assumed something no reasonable hidden-variable theory need obey) — a flaw the mathematician Grete Hermann had actually spotted decades earlier, to little notice.

Bohm's theory had one feature Bell couldn't stop staring at: its blatant nonlocality. So Bell asked the question that became his theorem — *was that nonlocality a defect of Bohm's particular construction, or was it forced?* Could someone cleverer have built a **local** hidden-variable theory instead? His theorem is the answer: **no.** Nonlocality is unavoidable. The theorem wasn't a weapon against Bohm; it was Bell proving that Bohm's most disturbing feature was not Bohm's fault but nature's. Bell went on to become, in Maudlin's words, pilot-wave theory's strongest advocate ever — stronger even than de Broglie or Bohm — and asked, pointedly, "Why is the pilot wave picture ignored in text books?"

### The bill Bohm does have to pay

None of this makes Bohm obviously *right* — it makes it *alive*, which is different, and honesty requires the real costs:

- **Relativity (the serious one).** Because the guiding equation needs the simultaneous configuration of distant particles, Bohm wants a preferred notion of "now" — a foliation of spacetime — and so is not manifestly Lorentz-invariant. Bell called this the real open problem, and it remains genuinely unfinished, though there's active work on covariant versions. (Caveat: since *every* single-world theory is nonlocal, some argue this tension is a cost of nature, not of Bohm specifically.)
- **But no faster-than-light signalling.** The nonlocal influence is uncontrollable: because you can never know a particle's actual position better than $|\psi|^2$ allows (Bohmians call this "absolute uncertainty"), the spooky effects average out and can't carry a message. So there's no operational conflict with relativity, no telegraph to the past — the nonlocality hides behind a statistical veil.
- **Ontology (taste).** The wave function living in high-dimensional configuration space, and the "empty" branches it keeps dragging around after a measurement, strike some people as extravagant. That's an aesthetic argument, not a refutation.

### What the experiments did and didn't do

The loophole-free Bell tests of 2015, and the 2022 Nobel Prize (Aspect, Clauser, Zeilinger), are sometimes reported as "proving quantum mechanics right and hidden variables wrong." What they actually did is close the experimental loopholes and confirm that **local** hidden-variable theories are dead. They say nothing against nonlocal theories. Bohm predicts precisely the quantum correlations those experiments measured — so every Bell-test result *vindicates* Bohm rather than threatening it. The theories left standing after 2015 are the nonlocal ones (Bohm), plus the more exotic escapes: superdeterminism (deny free choice), retrocausality (let the future setting reach back), and many-worlds (deny that measurements have single outcomes). Bohm takes the nonlocality horn and keeps everything else — including your free will to choose the settings.

So: like the pilot wave if you like the pilot wave. The thing you were told disqualifies it does the opposite. What Bell proved is that the universe is nonlocal, and the pilot-wave picture's one great virtue is that it says so out loud instead of hiding it in the word "measurement." That's not a reason to abandon it. It might be the best reason to take it seriously.
