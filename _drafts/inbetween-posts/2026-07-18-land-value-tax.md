Every tax you know does damage on its way to raising money. Tax income and people work a little less, or hide it. Tax sales and some trades that were worth making don't happen. Tax company profits and capital moves to where it isn't taxed. Economists call the wasted value — the trades killed by the tax, over and above the revenue collected — the *deadweight loss*, and it's the reason "efficient taxation" sounds like an oxymoron. Every tax is a wedge, and every wedge crushes something.

There is one exception, and it's been sitting in plain sight since 1879.

### The one thing you can't make less of

Henry George's observation in *Progress and Poverty* was simple. The deadweight loss of a tax comes from people *responding* to it — producing less of the taxed thing. So find something whose supply cannot respond, and tax that, and there is no distortion, because there is nothing anyone can do to shrink the taxed quantity.

Land — meaning *location*, the bare ground, not the buildings on it — is exactly that thing. You cannot manufacture more of central Auckland. The supply of it is a vertical line: perfectly fixed, no matter the price. Tax it, and the landowner cannot respond by producing less land, cannot move it offshore, cannot leave it in the ground. The quantity is unchanged, so no trade is killed, so the deadweight loss is **zero**. It is the only tax that is, in the technical sense, free.

> The geometry is worth seeing once. A tax's deadweight loss is a triangle whose size grows with how much the taxed quantity shrinks in response — the *elasticity* of supply. Land's supply elasticity is zero: the supply curve is vertical, the response is nil, and the triangle collapses to a line with no area. The entire burden of the tax falls on the landowner as lower rent, and *nothing else moves*. Every other tax you can name has a fat triangle; the land-value tax has none.

Milton Friedman — no one's idea of a soak-the-rich radical — called it "the least bad tax." That's the strange coalition Georgism attracts: it's endorsed across the political spectrum precisely because the efficiency argument is airtight and ideologically neutral.

### The part that should make you angry

Here's why it belongs on this blog and not just in an economics lecture. **Land value is not created by its owner.** It is created by everyone *else*.

Build a train station and the land near it jumps in value — and that gain flows to whoever happened to own the land, who did nothing but hold the deed while the public purse laid the track. Zone a neighbourhood, open a school, let a city grow up around a paddock, and the landowner captures an "unearned increment" — George's phrase — that the community, not the owner, produced. It's the purest example of the theme I keep returning to: [value that is luck, not merit]({{ site.baseurl }}/luck-morality). A location rent is a winning lottery ticket that keeps paying, drawn by the accident of what got built nearby, and we let the holder keep the whole pot.

A land-value tax reclaims that community-created value for the community that created it. It is the [spend-your-luck]({{ site.baseurl }}) principle turned into fiscal policy: you keep what you *build* (buildings and improvements go untaxed — in fact untaxing them encourages building), and you return what the *neighbourhood* handed you for free.

And there's a beautiful theorem lurking here that ties straight to [who should define the public good]({{ site.baseurl }}): the **Henry George Theorem** (Arnott & Stiglitz, 1977) proves that under fairly general conditions, the total land rent in a city *exactly equals* the optimal spending on its public goods. Public investment creates land value; land value can fund public investment; the books close on themselves. The thing that makes land valuable is precisely the thing land value could pay for.

### So why don't we?

If it's efficient *and* fair, the interesting question is why it's rare — and the answers are a catalogue of my favourite failure modes:

- **Measurement.** You have to separate the value of the *land* from the value of the *building* on it, parcel by parcel. It's a genuine assessment problem — the whole thing runs on being able to [measure the world]({{ site.baseurl }}) accurately, and valuation is hard and contestable.
- **Capitalised losses.** The tax is worth its whole future stream the day you announce it, so it crashes current land prices — hammering exactly the recent buyers and mortgage-holders who are least able to absorb it. The efficiency is long-run; the pain is a one-time shock to today's owners, who vote.
- **Power.** Landowners are concentrated, organised, and disproportionately hold political office. The beneficiaries — everyone who would pay less other tax — are diffuse and don't know it. It's the standard shape of [concentrated benefit versus diffuse cost]({{ site.baseurl }}/attention), run in reverse.

It isn't untried. Denmark, Estonia, Singapore, and Hong Kong lean on land rents; several Pennsylvania cities run "split-rate" systems that tax land more heavily than buildings. But the full George programme — fund the state from the unearned increment and stop taxing work — remains the great road not taken.

Which is the quiet scandal of it: we have known for nearly 150 years that there is a tax with no deadweight loss and a strong claim to justice, and we mostly don't levy it — not because the argument is wrong, but because the people who'd pay it own the ground the argument would have to be built on.
