For almost everything humans make, value equals use. A cancer drug earns more the more it is prescribed. A phone is worth building because people use phones. The market's basic reflex — reward what sells, build more of it — points in the right direction most of the time, which is why we mostly leave it alone.

Antibiotics run the reflex backwards, and the result is killing people right now.

### The inversion

Every time you use a new antibiotic, you teach bacteria to survive it. Use is not neutral; use is *depletion*. The drug's power is a finite, shared, exhaustible resource, and each dose spends a little of it forever. So a genuinely new antibiotic — one that works on the infections nothing else touches — is most valuable to society precisely when it is *sitting in a cabinet, unused*, held in reserve for the day the last-line drug fails. We want to conserve it. Conservation is the entire point of having it.

And conservation is exactly what destroys the revenue.

Look at the incentives an honest hospital faces. The responsible thing to do with a precious new antibiotic is to *not sell it* — reserve it, steward it, keep the volumes as low as clinically possible so resistance stays rare. The profitable thing to do with any product is to sell as much as possible. For antibiotics these two arrows point in opposite directions, and — correctly, ethically — stewardship programmes exist specifically to throttle the sales of the newest, most valuable drugs. We have built a market that pays a company for volume, and then built a second institution whose job is to suppress the volume of that company's best product. The better the drug, the harder we work to make sure nobody buys it.

### The graveyard

This is not a thought experiment. Achaogen got a genuinely novel antibiotic (plazomicin) through FDA approval in 2018 — and filed for bankruptcy in 2019, months later. Melinta Therapeutics, another dedicated antibiotics company, went bankrupt too. The big players — Novartis, AstraZeneca, Sanofi — have walked out of antibiotic R&D entirely. What's left of the pipeline is small biotechs that reliably go broke on approval, because approval is the moment they discover there's no way to make money selling a drug the world has agreed to hoard.

Meanwhile the resource keeps depleting. The best current estimate (the GRAM study, *Lancet* 2022) is roughly **1.27 million deaths in 2019 directly attributable to antibiotic resistance**, with about 5 million associated — already comparable to HIV and malaria. The O'Neill review's projection is **10 million deaths a year by 2050** if the trend holds. We are running low on the drugs that fight the thing set to kill more people than cancer, and the reason the cupboard is bare is that there is no money in the cure.

### Three failures, stacked

In the language I keep using here, this is not one market failure but three stacked on top of each other, and each is a case where the price signal *lies*:

1. **A positive externality.** Conserving a drug benefits everyone — the whole future population that will still have a working last line. The company that owns the drug captures none of that benefit. The value it creates and the value it can bill for have come apart.
2. **The value is option value.** What a reserve antibiotic is actually worth is the *right to use it later* — an insurance policy against a pandemic that hasn't happened. A volume-based market cannot price an option whose whole value is in not being exercised.
3. **Discovery is a public good.** Enormous fixed cost to find the molecule, near-zero marginal cost to make it, and society wants the knowledge to exist far more than any single firm can appropriate.

Three times over, the market builds what the price rewards rather than what we need. It is the [corporate alignment problem]({{ site.baseurl }}/alignment) in its purest form: the profit-maximiser is doing *exactly what we told it to do*. We told it wrong.

### The fix has a shape

You fix an inverted incentive by cutting the wire that inverts it. **Delink revenue from volume.** Pay a company for *access to* a working antibiotic, not for *doses of* it.

The UK did this first, and it is the part of the story that gives me hope. In 2022 the NHS, via NICE, ran a "subscription" model — a fixed annual fee (up to roughly £10 million a year) for two antibiotics, paid whether the NHS uses one dose or a million. It values the drug the way you value insurance, or an option, which is what it is. The US PASTEUR Act proposes the same mechanism at national scale — a government subscription, a market-entry reward decoupled from sales — and has stalled in Congress for years. The distinction that matters: *push* funding (BARDA, CARB-X) pays for the research; *pull* funding (subscriptions, prizes) pays for the finished drug regardless of how little it sells. You need the pull, because push alone just builds more drugs that no one can afford to bring to market.

And notice what makes the fix hard, because it is the throughline of half of what I write: you can only pay for the option if you can *value* the option — put a number on the pandemic that didn't happen. That is the same measurement problem as pricing [avoided carbon]({{ site.baseurl }}/the-future-of-environmental-sciences), avoided extinction, avoided kleptocracy. The value lives entirely in a counterfactual, and markets are blind to counterfactuals until a mechanism drags the number into the daylight.

We have decided — not through any vote, but through the plain structure of the market — that we would rather have a fourth statin than a new antibiotic. Nobody chose that. It fell out of the incentives, the way water finds the low ground. And the bacteria are counting on us not noticing in time.
