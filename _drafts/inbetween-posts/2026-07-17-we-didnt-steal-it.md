There is a sentence that no government, museum, or company ever says out loud, but which sits underneath a remarkable number of official statements about colonial history:

> *"We know X was stolen from you. We didn't personally steal it. And we quite like having X."*

Once you have the sentence, you start seeing it everywhere. It is the actual content of the "acknowledgment" genre — the renovated museum wing, the carefully worded statement of regret, the plaque. The Royal Museum for Central Africa in Tervuren, built with Leopold II's Congo profits, reopened in 2018 after a five-year "decolonisation": critical wall texts, commissioned Congolese counter-art, the most racist statues moved into a visible depot. What didn't move was the collection — roughly 85% of it acquired during the colonial period. The labels changed hands. The inventory didn't.

And the language is engineered. When the Belgian king addressed Congo in 2020 and again in Kinshasa in 2022, he offered his "deepest regrets" — pointedly not an apology. A Belgian parliamentary commission on the colonial past later collapsed in part over that exact word, reportedly because a formal apology might ground legal liability<sup>1</sup>. Sit with that for a moment: the distinction between *regret* and *apology* exists in diplomatic language precisely because one is compensation-proof and the other might not be. Acknowledgment isn't a step toward restitution. It is built to be a terminus.

## Guilt dies with the person. Enrichment doesn't.

The reason "we didn't steal it" *feels* like a strong answer is that it's true, and it's answering a question about guilt. Nobody alive committed the Banda massacre or ran Leopold's rubber regime. If the claim against the present is "you are guilty," the present can honestly answer "no."

But that was never the claim. The claim is "you are *holding*." And on holding, our own ordinary law is unambiguous: you cannot inherit an estate's assets and disclaim its debts. You may refuse an inheritance entirely, but you cannot cherry-pick. This isn't radical politics; it's probate logic. Colonial wealth is an estate with an unadjudicated debt attached, and the heirs have been cherry-picking for centuries.

What's striking is that this conclusion survives from both ends of political philosophy. Egalitarians object to large inheritances in general. But even Robert Nozick — the most property-friendly serious philosopher available, the libertarians' libertarian — built his theory of just holdings on three principles: just acquisition, just transfer, and *rectification* of past injustice. He conceded that given how much actual history consists of theft, rectification might justify sweeping redistribution<sup>2</sup>. There is no serious theory of property on which stolen-then-inherited wealth comes out clean. The disagreement was never really philosophical.

## The real blocker is measurement

So why does compensation talk always die? Because the honest version of the compensatory question is unanswerable. What would the Congo be today, absent Leopold? What would the descendants of the enslaved have, absent slavery? These counterfactuals cannot be constructed, and beneath them sits a genuine philosophical trapdoor — Parfit's non-identity problem: most people alive today exist *only because* history went the way it did<sup>3</sup>. Different history, different people. "You would have been better off" is, at the level of individuals, incoherent.

Defenders of the status quo have learned to live in this trapdoor. Any concrete reparations number can be met with "but how was it calculated?", and since every calculation of a counterfactual loss is contestable, the conversation resets to zero. Add the opportunity-cost point — the harm isn't the nominal value of what was taken, but everything it would have compounded into — and the number gets bigger *and* softer at the same time. Unmeasurable claims are easy to owe.

But there is a flip available, and it's an old one in law: when the victim's loss can't be measured, measure the beneficiary's *retained gain* instead. Lawyers call it disgorgement — the remedy for unjust enrichment. You don't ask "what did they lose?" (uncomputable). You ask "what are you still holding?" — and retained gains, unlike counterfactual losses, leave ledgers.

This is the whole point: **the stolen wealth is traceable in a way the destroyed futures are not.** Leopold's Congo profits — on the order of 220 million francs of the day<sup>4</sup> — went into the Cinquantenaire arch, the Tervuren museum, the Ostend seafront, and the Donation Royale, a royal property trust that exists today as a named legal entity. The mining company he chartered in 1906, Union Minière du Haut-Katanga, has a direct corporate descendant listed on the Brussels exchange. The Dutch state didn't just charter the VOC; it *inherited* it in 1799, assets and debts, and in some mid-nineteenth-century years the surplus remitted from the Indies made up as much as a third of Dutch state revenue<sup>5</sup>. None of this is hidden. It just hasn't been added up, in public, with names attached.

## The audit works. We've run the experiment three times.

**1. Morel and the manifests.** In the late 1890s, a shipping clerk in his twenties named E. D. Morel noticed something in the cargo records of the Liverpool line holding the Congo shipping monopoly: ships arrived from the Congo full of rubber and ivory worth millions, and sailed back carrying almost nothing but rifles, ammunition, and soldiers. No trade goods. Nothing the rubber could have been *bought* with. From the ledgers alone he deduced that the "trade" was extraction by force — and the resulting Congo Reform Association became arguably the first modern human-rights campaign<sup>6</sup>. Leopold's regime survived missionaries' atrocity photographs for years; what it could not survive was an accountant. The humanitarian facade was a narrative, and narratives can absorb testimony. They cannot absorb arithmetic.

**2. The slave-owners' compensation database.** When Britain abolished slavery in 1833, it paid £20 million in compensation — about 40% of the government's annual budget — to the slave *owners*. The enslaved received nothing. For 180 years this was a footnote. Then, in 2013, researchers at UCL published the complete compensation records as a searchable public database: every claimant, by name, with what they were paid and for how many people<sup>7</sup>. The abstraction "Britain profited from slavery" became "here is the ledger entry for this bank, this university, this family." Named institutions — Lloyd's of London, Greene King, the Bank of England — were pushed into apologies and research programmes not by a new moral argument but by an old spreadsheet. And when the Treasury cheerfully tweeted in 2018 that taxpayers had helped "end the slave trade" by servicing the compensation debt until 2015, the database was why the public could immediately understand what it was actually reading: living Britons had been paying off the slave-owners' payout within the decade.

**3. Haiti's double debt.** In 1825 France demanded that Haiti compensate the former slaveholders for the loss of their "property" — the Haitians themselves — as the price of recognition, at gunpoint. Haiti borrowed from French banks to pay France, and serviced the resulting "double debt" into the twentieth century. Everyone vaguely knew this. In 2022 the *New York Times* did the accounting: traced the payments, named the banks (including the ancestor of one of France's largest), and computed the compounded cost to Haiti's development at somewhere between $21 billion and $115 billion<sup>8</sup>. Within three years, France — which had spent two centuries not discussing it — announced a joint commission of historians on the indemnity's legacy. Not restitution. But the question had permanently changed from *whether* to *how much*, which is the one transition "we didn't steal it" cannot survive.

The pattern in all three: no new facts were discovered. The facts were always in the records, often in the perpetrators' own meticulous ledgers. What changed was that someone did the accounting *publicly enough* — with names, numbers, and present-day balances — that the general became specific. "We didn't steal it" works against an abstraction. It does not work against an itemised statement of what "it" is and where it currently sits.

## Separate the finding from the remedy

Here is the modest proposal buried in all this. The reparations debate stalls because it tries to settle three questions at once — what happened, who holds the proceeds now, and what should be done — and opponents of the third can therefore prevent the first two from ever being answered. So separate them.

A court of record — or failing that, a research programme, a database, a newsroom — whose only outputs are *findings of fact* and an *accounting*: what was taken, and a tracing of where it went, ending in named entities that exist today. No damages. No defendants in the dock. Just the ledger, published. This is deliberately weak, and that is its strength: every argument deployed against reparations — "it was long ago," "I didn't do it," "the sum is incalculable" — is an argument against a *remedy*, and none of them is an argument against an *audit*. Opposing the remedy is a position. Opposing the audit is a confession.

Would the accounting, by itself, deliver justice? No. Findings without enforcement are findings without enforcement. But the three experiments above suggest something I find hopeful, in a bleak sort of way: the public ledger is upstream of everything else. The apologies, the restitution laws, the commissions — in each case they followed the arithmetic, not the moral argument, because the moral argument had been available (and ignored) for a century. And there is a precedent for the money eventually moving too: the German foundation that paid €5 billion to 1.6 million former forced labourers in the 2000s was funded jointly by the state and several thousand named companies — companies that had spent fifty years explaining that the guilty were dead<sup>9</sup>. What changed wasn't their guilt. It was that the accounting had made their enrichment undeniable.

Guilt dies with the person. Enrichment doesn't — it compounds, and it keeps books. The books are still there. Somebody just has to read them out loud.
