title: No Fault
subtitle: A woman who stopped believing anyone was to blame — including herself

Vera was, everyone agreed, the most merciful judge in the district, and nobody could work out why it made them so uneasy.

She had arrived at it honestly, the way you arrive at the bottom of a proof. She had spent thirty years watching people be marched into her courtroom and asked to account for themselves, and she had come to understand that not one of them had authored the self they were accounting for. The man who beat someone half to death had a father who beat him, and a father's father before that, and a birth on the wrong side of a street he did not choose, and a particular density of receptors in a particular fold of a brain he had not designed. Follow any action back and it dissolved into causes, and the causes into causes, and nowhere in the regress was there a small clean room where a free man sat and *decided* to beat his wife to death.

So she stopped blaming people. Not as a policy — she simply lost the ability, the way you can lose the ability to see a face in a pattern once you've seen it's only dots. To blame a man for flooding was to blame a river. You did not hate the river. You built the levee, you moved the town, you got the people out of the water. You looked forward, because forward was the only direction in which anything could still be done.

For a while this made her wise. She sentenced to protect and to deter and to repair, never to punish for the sweetness of punishment, and the people who came before her felt, often for the first time, *seen* — seen all the way down, past the crime to the machine that had produced it. Reformers wrote about her. She thought they had rather missed the point, but she let them.

The symmetry took longer to arrive, and it arrived at home.

If no one could be blamed, then no one could be credited. This followed with the same force; she could not accept the one and refuse the other without lying, and she did not lie. Her daughter's kindness was the daughter's causes running their course — lovely to watch, like weather, and no more *hers* than a sunset is the sky's achievement. Vera could not be proud of it. She tried. Pride required an author, and she had proven there were none.

Her husband she could not thank. This was the one that ended things. For forty years he had chosen her, every ordinary morning, and one night she told him — gently, because it was only the truth and the truth is not cruel — that she could not be grateful for it, because he could not have done otherwise. He had been *going* to stay, the way a stone is going to fall. To thank him would be to congratulate the stone.

*Just pretend,* he said. *You don't have to believe it. Just say it and let me have it.*

She couldn't. Pretending was a lie, and she was, above all the other things she had stopped being, still honest. He left in the spring. She noticed that she felt no grief, only the flat recognition of an outcome: he had always been going to leave a woman who saw him as weather. She was weather too. Two fronts, meeting and parting. She signed the papers. There was a box on them, she remembered, for the grounds. *No fault.* She had thought at the time that it was the only honest phrase the law had ever produced.

The last thing happened four years later, and it is the reason I am telling you about her.

A man came before her whom she knew — knew the way you know a proof is going to close — to be dangerous. And there was a seam in the case, a technicality she could have ruled around with a sentence, and she did not rule around it. She let the seam do its work. She would tell you, if you asked, that she made no *decision*; that she watched herself not rule around it the way she had watched forty years of defendants do the things their causes made them do. He walked out. Some weeks later he did the thing she had known he would do, to someone who had done nothing, and there was an inquiry, and they sat the merciful judge down and asked her how *she*, of all people, could have let it happen.

And she gave them the answer she had given about every soul who had ever stood in her dock. She said: *I was never going to do otherwise. It was always going to happen this way.*

And the terrible thing — the thing that should frighten you, because it frightens me — is that she was right. The philosophy that had reached out and forgiven the whole shabby, battered, undeserving world finally reached back and forgave *her*, for the one thing that had ever actually been hers to carry. She had proven, years ago, in a courtroom, past all doubt, that guilt was incoherent. So she felt none. There was nowhere for it to attach.

They asked her to sign the finding. She read it to the bottom, where the box was, and where someone had already typed the two words, and she found that after everything they were still the only honest phrase the law had ever produced.

*No fault.*

She signed it, the way a stone signs its falling, and felt, exactly as she had proven she would, nothing at all.
