## Premise

At Nuremberg the powers invented machinery for judging state crime — and immediately made sure it would never point backwards, or at themselves. In this timeline, the smaller powers win one concession in the 1946–48 negotiations that produced the Genocide Convention: alongside the (real-history-proposed, real-history-strangled) permanent criminal court, a **Chamber for Historical Crimes**.

The Chamber is designed to be toothless — which is exactly why the great powers allow it to exist. It cannot punish anyone: its defendants are dead. It can only do two things:

1. Make **findings of fact** — an authoritative record of what happened.
2. Issue an **Accounting** — a tracing of where the proceeds of a historical crime went, and where they now sit.

A court of record, not of punishment. Harmless symbolism, the great powers assume.

Its first petition is filed in 1947 by the **Republic of Indonesia** — a state that does not yet legally exist, mid-war-of-independence against the Netherlands — against the **Vereenigde Oostindische Compagnie**, dissolved 1799, for the destruction of the Banda Islands in 1621.

**The engine of the story: the Chamber works. That is why it is killed.**

## Point of divergence

Small and plausible: during the Genocide Convention negotiations, the bloc of small and newly independent states (India, Ethiopia, Haiti, Liberia, the Latin American republics) trades its votes for the Historical Chamber. The great powers accept because a court that cannot punish looks like a court that cannot matter. Nobody at the table has thought carefully about what an *accounting* is.

## POV characters (alternating)

- **The archivist (Dutch):** a young state archivist seconded to the *defense* team to find exculpatory documents in the VOC archives. He keeps finding the opposite. His arc mirrors E. D. Morel's: the atrocity is legible in the ledgers, and he is the one person in the room trained to read them. Decision point: what does an archivist owe — his employer, or the record?
- **The junior counsel (Indonesian):** a law student in The Hague, working for the Republic's delegation, whose family traces to the Banda diaspora. She understands from the start what the Dutch team learns too late: the Republic isn't trying to win money. It's trying to make a *record* — while the Netherlands is actively fighting to retake the archipelago.

## Beats

1. **The concession.** 1946–47, the Chamber created as a sop. Housed, with grim irony, in The Hague. Staffed by judges the great powers consider safely unimportant.
2. **The petition.** Indonesia files. First fight is standing: can a state that doesn't yet exist petition about a company that no longer exists? The Chamber's ruling — that the Chamber exists precisely for parties and defendants who fall outside ordinary law — is the moment it stops being symbolic.
3. **The defense.** The Netherlands appears "voluntarily" (to control the narrative) and runs three arguments: (a) the VOC was a company, not the state; (b) it was dissolved in 1799; (c) "standards of the time."
4. **Standards of the time, refuted by the time.** The prosecution's answer to (c) is the archivist's first find: in 1621 the Heeren XVII *themselves* censured Coen for the Banda operation — "we would have wished it had been done by milder means" — **and then paid him a 3,000-guilder bonus**. The censure and the bonus sit in the same ledger. The era knew. It paid anyway. (This is real.)
5. **The estate was accepted.** The answer to (a) and (b): the 1798–1800 nationalization documents. The Dutch state took the VOC's assets *and its debts*. Prosecution's line, and maybe the story's thesis: *"You cannot inherit the estate and disclaim its debts. The estate was accepted."*
6. **The witness.** An elder from Banda Eli, in the Kei Islands — where descendants of the 1621 survivors still live and still keep the Bandanese language (real). Testimony of transmitted memory: the names of the forty-four orang kaya executed by Coen's Japanese mercenaries, kept for three centuries. The courtroom hears names the archive doesn't hold. Record meets memory; each has what the other lacks.
7. **The collision.** December 1947: news of the Rawagedeh massacre — Dutch troops executing villagers in Java *during the trial*. Prosecution moves to read the newspaper into the record. Defense objects: beyond the Chamber's temporal scope. Judge: "Counsel's objection is that the crime is too recent. The Chamber has until now only heard the objection that crimes are too old."
8. **The finding and the Accounting.** The Chamber finds the facts of 1621 proven and issues its Accounting: the 1799 absorption of VOC assets into the Dutch state; the colonial surplus (the *batig slot*) remitted from the Indies to the Dutch treasury through the nineteenth century — railways, debt retirement. It orders nothing it can enforce. It computes a sum and "leaves it with the parties." The number is published. That is all — and it is enough to change the meaning of every guilder in the Dutch budget.
9. **The flood, and the strangling.** Petitions pour in: Herero delegates against Germany; Congolese claimants against the Leopoldine estate and the Donation Royale; a slavery petition against several powers at once; Bengal (the 1770 famine) against the East India Company — and both the US and USSR discover their own exposure. 1950: the statute is quietly amended; the Chamber is "suspended pending procedural review," sine die. The Chamber is not killed for failing. It is killed for working.
10. **Epilogue (decades later).** The Chamber's archive sits in The Hague — findings without enforcement. In 2011, lawyers for the widows of Rawagedeh cite the VOC finding in a Dutch court, and win (the real Rawagedeh judgment, with one extra citation). Closing image: the reading room; the wrongs, written.

## Themes

- Guilt dies with the person; enrichment doesn't. The trial is about holding, not guilt.
- "Standards of the time" as a defense refuted by the time's own documents.
- The archive as the only verdict actually available — and whether that is nothing or everything.
- A justice institution destroyed by its own success (mirrors the real fifty-year strangling of the ICC between 1948 and 1998).

## For the afterword (real history to carry)

- VOC charter of 1602: delegated rights to wage war, make treaties, build forts, coin money east of the Cape.
- Banda, 1621: population ~15,000 killed, enslaved, or deported under Coen; 44 orang kaya executed; islands repopulated as slave-worked *perkenier* plantations. The Heeren XVII censure + bonus.
- 1799: VOC nationalized — Dutch state assumes assets and debts; runs the Indies as a state colony until 1949.
- Genocide Convention art. VI envisioned an international penal tribunal; the Cold War killed it; ICC arrives 2002, *without* retroactive jurisdiction.
- Indonesian independence war and the "police actions"; Rawagedeh (9 Dec 1947); Dutch court holds the state liable, 2011.
- Banda Eli, Kei Islands: the surviving Bandanese-speaking diaspora community.
- Coen: "we cannot make war without trade, nor trade without war."

## Open questions

- One POV or two? (Two feels right: the ledger and the memory need separate carriers.)
- Does the Chamber compute an actual number on the page? (Tempting: a specific, absurdly large, defensible figure — the reader should feel the number's weight.)
- Tone of the epilogue: the 2011 citation is hopeful; is hope earned here, or should it stay at "the record survives"?
- Length: this wants to be the longest of the set — possibly serialized chapters like Wai-terra.
