This is the empirical entry point to a four-part series on collective decision-making, agency, and alignment. The other posts work the philosophy and the formal apparatus: the [procedural framework](/governance-mechanisms/), the [definition of agency](/governance-mechanisms/agency/), the [mechanism-design problem](/governance-mechanisms/mechanism-design/), and the [alignment-pursuit machinery](/governance-mechanisms/alignment/). This post answers a different question — *why should I care about any of that?* — and the answer is empirical, not philosophical.

The short version: the historical record shows that institutional design has enormous welfare consequences. Two populations with the same people, geography, technology, and natural resources can produce wildly different outcomes depending on the rules under which they collectively decide. Most contemporary AI safety work assumes that the institutions inside which AI is developed are roughly fixed, and that the work is to make AI safe *within* them. The historical record suggests this assumption is the most consequential and most under-examined move in the field.

## 1. The natural experiments

Most empirical questions in economics and political science are hard because you can't run the experiment. You can't take a country, copy it, run the copy under different rules, and compare outcomes after fifty years. Except sometimes you can — when history has accidentally produced the experiment for you.

**The Korean peninsula.** In 1945, Korea was a single ethnically and culturally homogeneous population with shared language, history, and pre-war economic conditions. Three years later it was divided arbitrarily at the 38th parallel into two states with sharply different political and economic institutions. Eighty years later, South Korea has a per-capita GDP roughly 25–30 times that of North Korea. Population health, life expectancy, infant mortality, literacy, infrastructure quality — every measurable indicator shows the same massive divergence. Same people. Same geography. Different rules.

**Nogales.** The city of Nogales sits across the US–Mexico border. The same families live on both sides; the cultural and geographical conditions are essentially identical. The institutional environment is not — different legal systems, different property-rights regimes, different access to public goods. Income per capita differs by roughly a factor of three between the two sides of the same town.

**Colonial origins.** Acemoglu, Johnson, and Robinson (2001) used the historical record of European colonisation to compare outcomes between colonies that established inclusive institutions (broad property rights, representative government, rule of law extending to settlers) and colonies that established extractive institutions (concentrated land ownership, coerced labour, racial hierarchy, rule by colonial elite). The colonies founded on inclusive institutions — North America, Australia, New Zealand — converged toward broad-based prosperity. The colonies founded on extractive institutions — much of Latin America, sub-Saharan Africa, the Caribbean — did not.

Each of these is a controlled comparison where the institutions are the variable and the outcomes are the measurement. The combined evidence — across many such comparisons — is the empirical case for the claim that institutional design dominates most other commonly-invoked explanations for why some societies prosper and others don't.

## 2. Inclusive vs extractive

Acemoglu and Robinson's working distinction is between *inclusive* and *extractive* institutions. The 2024 Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences (shared with Simon Johnson) recognised this framework.

**Extractive institutions** concentrate economic and political power in a narrow elite. The elite uses its position to extract resources from the broader population. Property rights extend reliably only to the elite; the rule of law is selective; political participation is restricted; entry into productive activities requires elite permission.

**Inclusive institutions** broaden participation. Property rights extend to a wide population. The rule of law is general. Political participation is open. New entrants can innovate without requiring permission from incumbents. Economic and political power are not concentrated in the same hands, so each can constrain the other.

The two kinds of institutions are *self-reinforcing*. Extractive institutions concentrate power, which the holders then use to maintain extractive institutions. Inclusive institutions disperse power, which the dispersed beneficiaries use to resist concentration. Transitions from extractive to inclusive are historically rare and often violent — the Glorious Revolution of 1688 in England is the canonical example, preceding the Industrial Revolution by roughly a century.

A crucial empirical regularity: extractive institutions can produce growth for a while. The Soviet Union industrialised rapidly under extractive institutions in the 1920s and 1930s. South Korea was extractive under Park Chung-hee. China has lifted 800 million people out of extreme poverty since 1980 under a one-party state. But extractive growth has a ceiling — it works while the elite can identify the things worth investing in (steel, railways, basic manufacturing), but it stalls when the next stage of growth requires *creative destruction* that threatens the elite's position. Innovation, by definition, redistributes power. Elites under extractive institutions face a choice between economic dynamism and political control. Historically, they have mostly chosen control.

## 3. Why "inclusive" means "you have a say"

The crucial property of inclusive institutions, for the purposes of this series, is that they admit broad inputs to the procedure that determines collective behaviour. Inclusion is not a moral category in this telling — it is an information-flow category. *You being included* means that *your preferences enter the aggregation procedure that determines the rules*. *You being excluded* means they don't.

The empirical record shows that excluded preferences don't get served. Extractive elites do not voluntarily invest in the long-run welfare of populations whose preferences are not entering the procedure. They invest in their own welfare. Sometimes this overlaps with broad welfare — early-stage industrialisation, basic public health — but the overlap shrinks as the easy gains are exhausted, and the divergence between elite welfare and broad welfare grows as time goes on.

This is the empirical version of the same argument the rest of the series makes formally. [Posts 0–3](/governance-mechanisms/) argue that the mechanism determines the implicit objective; the implicit objective determines long-run outcomes; therefore designing the mechanism is the most important upstream problem. The empirical literature on institutions is the historical evidence for that theoretical claim. The Korean peninsula and Nogales and the colonial-origins comparison are the natural experiments showing that *which mechanism* determines *which outcomes*, at a scale that dominates almost everything else we might worry about.

## 4. The hard case: China

The most challenging empirical case for the inclusive-institutions thesis is contemporary China. Since roughly 1980, China has lifted approximately 800 million people out of extreme poverty under a one-party state. This is the largest welfare gain in human history measured by people lifted from material deprivation. The political institutions are unambiguously extractive in Acemoglu and Robinson's sense — concentrated political power, restricted participation, opaque decision-making, no rule of law constraining party leadership.

How do you square this with the inclusive-institutions thesis?

Three responses, in increasing order of strength.

*First*, the Chinese welfare gain has been mostly catching-up: applying technologies and management practices developed elsewhere, in a population kept artificially poor by extractive institutions in the prior period (the Maoist state was straightforwardly extractive). Catching-up growth is what extractive institutions can do. The test is whether dynamic, innovation-driven growth at the frontier is sustainable under extractive institutions. The historical record is mostly negative on that question — the Soviet Union, Argentina, and several others stalled at the catch-up frontier — but China hasn't yet hit the frontier itself, so we don't know.

*Second*, the Chinese case includes substantial *partial inclusion* — broad participation in economic activity (millions of small businesses, market entry and exit, property rights of a sort extending to the urban middle class) even as political participation is restricted. Acemoglu and Robinson treat this configuration as inherently unstable: economic inclusion creates a middle class with preferences that political exclusion cannot accommodate, and either the political institutions open up or the economic dynamism shuts down. We do not yet know which way the Chinese case resolves.

*Third*, the welfare gain is real but its sustainability depends on whether the institutions that produced it remain functional through the transition to the frontier. The 1989 collapse of the Soviet system is the cautionary example: forty years of catching-up growth that, at the frontier, could not transition to inclusive institutions and instead collapsed.

None of these is a refutation of the China case. China may produce a counter-example to the inclusive-institutions thesis — that's a live possibility. The honest framing is that the institutional thesis is the best-supported hypothesis in the historical record so far, that China is the strongest contemporary challenge to it, and that the next twenty years will tell us which way the empirical evidence goes.

## 5. The AI institutional choice

The historical record matters for the AI case because we are now making, as a species, an institutional choice as consequential as any in the historical record. The institutions inside which frontier AI is being developed — the rules governing labs, the corporate forms in which they sit, the regulatory environment around them, the procedures by which models acquire their objectives — are being established right now, and they will be locked in by path-dependence for a long time.

Several near-term institutional decisions are visible:

- Whether AI labs are governed by shareholder primacy or by a broader stakeholder structure.
- Whether model weights and training data are owned by the labs that produce them, or are subject to public-interest constraints.
- Whether the procedures that determine model objectives (RLHF, Constitutional AI) include input from outside lab leadership.
- Whether compute provision is structurally separable from model development.
- Whether the regulatory bodies overseeing AI are independent or industry-aligned.
- Whether AI labs are subject to criminal liability for systemic harms produced by their systems.

Each of these is a *rule* in the sense the rest of this series uses the word. Each is being set right now. The combination determines the implicit objective of frontier AI development, in the same way that the combination of shareholder primacy, limited liability, fiduciary duty, and board structure determines the implicit objective of the modern corporation.

The historical record suggests that the choice between inclusive and extractive AI institutions will dominate almost everything else we might worry about — choice of training algorithm, capability evaluation method, interpretability technique. The pursuit machinery from [Post 3](/governance-mechanisms/alignment/) is necessary but downstream. The institutional substrate is upstream.

## 6. What the historical record cannot tell us

The historical record does not provide a finished theory. It is suggestive evidence, not proof. Several limits matter for this series:

- The natural experiments are small in number. North/South Korea and Nogales are striking, but two cases cannot pin down a general theory on their own.
- The institutional categories ("inclusive", "extractive") are coarse. Two countries scored "inclusive" on a binary measure can differ enormously on specific institutional features.
- Contemporary China is the strongest live counter-example, and its resolution is not yet known.
- The historical record predates machine-speed optimisation. The institutional dynamics of pre-AI societies may not all transplant cleanly.

The work of the rest of the series is to take this empirical suggestion and produce a formal framework that lets us reason about specific institutional designs in specific conditions, including the new condition of machine-speed optimisation. The historical record motivates the work; it does not finish it.

## 7. Where this lands

If you are persuaded that institutional design matters enormously, the framework starts at [Post 0](/governance-mechanisms/) and the rest of the series follows. [Post 1](/governance-mechanisms/agency/) defines a candidate target for institutional design (*agency* — the system's capacity to enable its members to pursue their goals). [Post 2](/governance-mechanisms/mechanism-design/) is the specification problem: how do we design the mechanism that aggregates preferences? [Post 3](/governance-mechanisms/alignment/) is the pursuit problem: given a specified target, how do we build machinery that pursues it without drift? The companion [derivation post on agency](/governance-mechanisms/agency-derivation/) walks the dead ends behind the candidate target.

If you are not persuaded — if you think institutional choice is downstream of more important variables — the rest of the series is a different exercise from the one you want to be running. I do not expect to persuade you here. The empirical literature has decades of work on this; I'm pointing at it, not summarising it. Acemoglu and Robinson's *Why Nations Fail* (2012) and *The Narrow Corridor* (2019) are the canonical popular treatments. Putnam's *Making Democracy Work* (1993) covers a different angle (civic capital and its institutional consequences). North, Wallis, and Weingast (2009), *Violence and Social Orders*, provide a more abstract framework (limited-access vs open-access orders).

The argument of this series, taken as a whole, is that the institutions humans use to make collective decisions are the most consequential things humans build, that they have been built badly across most of history and are being built badly now, that we have the formal apparatus to do better, and that the AI moment is the moment when getting this right matters most.
