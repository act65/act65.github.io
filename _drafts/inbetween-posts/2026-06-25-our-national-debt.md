Years ago I came across [nationaldebtclocks.org](https://www.nationaldebtclocks.org/debtclock/newzealand) and found myself hypnotized. The number just keeps going up, fast. I did some napkin maths, got annoyed, scribbled a page of questions, and then — as one does — let it sit for the better part of a decade.

I've finally gone back to answer them. The numbers have only gotten bigger.

## The napkin maths, updated

When I first looked, I worked out that every New Zealander was paying somewhere around \$1,000 a year in interest on the national debt. Here's where we are now.

At 30 June 2025, **net core Crown debt was \$182.2 billion — about 41.8% of GDP** ([Treasury, Financial Statements of the Government 2025](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/year-end/financial-statements-2025)). The government's finance costs — the interest bill — ran to roughly **\$8.9 billion** for the core Crown that year ([Budget 2025 fiscal context](https://www.budget.govt.nz/budget/2025/fiscal-strategy-report/fiscal-context.htm)). With a population of about [5.32 million](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/national-population-estimates-at-30-june-2025/), that's **~\$1,670 in interest per person, per year**. More than we spend on defence. More than we spend on housing.

For comparison, back in 2018 net debt was \$57.5 billion (19.9% of GDP) and the interest bill was around \$3.5 billion — roughly \$720 a head. So in seven years the debt has **roughly tripled** and per-capita interest has more than doubled. The bulk of that jump is COVID: the government was running surpluses and sitting near 19% of GDP in 2019, then borrowed enormously to fund the pandemic response. Net debt rose by something like \$118 billion in five years ([Treasury, Financial Statements 2024](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/year-end/financial-statements-2024)).

A quick note on which number you're looking at, because they differ wildly and people quote whichever suits them:

- **Net core Crown debt** (~\$182bn) — the headline measure; borrowings minus financial assets, and it deliberately *excludes* the Super Fund.
- **Gross debt** (~\$213bn) — borrowings before netting anything off.
- **Total Crown liabilities** (~\$409bn) — everything on the balance sheet, including ACC and EQC insurance liabilities.

I'll use net core Crown debt below, since that's the one the rules and the politicians revolve around.[^measures]

## Why are we even in debt?

This was my first and most naive question. Why does a government — which can literally tax people and, in extremis, print money — need to borrow from investors at all?

The short answer is **deficit smoothing**. The government funds most of its spending from taxes, but in any year where spending exceeds revenue, the gap is filled by borrowing ([NZDM, *Government Borrowing — the Basics*](https://debtmanagement.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/media/media_attachment/nzdm-borrowingbasics-aug20.pdf)). The deeper logic is **tax smoothing**: taxes are economically distorting, and the distortion grows faster than the tax rate. So it's less damaging to borrow through a bad year — a war, an earthquake, a pandemic — and repay gradually than to spike taxes brutally when the shock hits and slash them when it passes ([Treasury Analytical Note AN 24/07](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-08/an24-07.pdf)). Debt lets you spread a one-off cost across many years, and arguably across generations.

*(Why not just print the money instead? Because that tends to convert the debt into inflation rather than make it disappear — which is exactly why NZ keeps the two jobs separate: Treasury borrows, while the independent [Reserve Bank](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/monetary-policy/monetary-policy-tools/large-scale-asset-purchase-programme) targets inflation.)*

> *An aside I'd wondered about: how is this different from microfinance, which feels unambiguously good?* Almost entirely. A microloan averages [a few hundred US dollars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microfinance), goes to an individual with no collateral and no other access to credit, and carries an eye-watering interest rate (global averages around 35%). NZ borrows billions, is backed by the power to tax and a near-top credit rating, and pays single-digit interest. The only thing they share is the word "loan".

## What is a government bond, and who would buy one?

A New Zealand Government Bond is about as boring a financial instrument as exists, which is the point. You lend the Crown money; it pays you a fixed coupon twice a year, calculated on the face value; and on the maturity date you get the face value back ([NZDM information memorandum](https://debtmanagement.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/Information_Memorandum_-_Nominal_Bonds.pdf)). Issuance is run by [New Zealand Debt Management](https://debtmanagement.treasury.govt.nz/about-us), a unit inside Treasury, which auctions bonds weekly to a handful of big "primary dealer" banks, who then sell them on. (There's also a retail version, [Kiwi Bonds](https://debtmanagement.treasury.govt.nz/kiwi-bonds), if you personally want to lend the government money — it's basically a term deposit.)

Why would anyone buy them? Because they're the safest NZ-dollar asset there is, backed by the Crown's taxing power and a sovereign credit rating of [AA+ / AAA](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/576011/global-rating-agency-reaffirms-nz-high-level-credit-rating). As of mid-2026 they yield roughly 3.3% at two years out to about 4.4% at ten years. Not exciting — but safe and liquid, which is exactly what pension funds, insurers and central banks want as ballast.

*Who actually holds our debt, then?* This was the question I most wanted answered, and the answer is a little uncomfortable: as of mid-2025, **around 60% of New Zealand government bonds were held offshore** ([NZDM securities overview](https://debtmanagement.treasury.govt.nz/resource/new-zealand-government-securities-overview-2025-26)). That share isn't fixed — historically it's swung anywhere from under 50% to around 80% — but ~60% is roughly where it sits today.

So who *are* they? On the offshore side, mostly the unglamorous institutions that park money in safe sovereign debt: foreign central banks and reserve managers, global bond funds, insurers. There's no single dramatic creditor — no one country or fund "owns" us; it's thousands of institutions each holding a slice. Onshore, the other ~40% is held by our own banks, fund and superannuation managers, and insurers, plus a sizeable chunk the [Reserve Bank](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/monetary-policy/monetary-policy-tools/large-scale-asset-purchase-programme) bought during its COVID-era bond-buying programme and is now slowly selling back. Worth knowing how it all gets out there in the first place: NZDM doesn't sell to the public, it auctions to a handful of approved "primary dealer" banks each week, and *they* distribute the bonds onward into this wider pool.

## Where does the interest go?

Follow the logic of that 60%. Interest paid to a *domestic* bondholder stays in the country — your cost is some other New Zealander's income; it nets out. But interest paid to an *offshore* holder genuinely leaves: it's a transfer abroad, the thing that turns GDP into the smaller Gross National Income.

Treasury doesn't publish a single "interest paid overseas" line, so this is my estimate rather than an official figure — but if roughly 60% of the bonds are held offshore, then **the majority of that ~\$8.9 billion interest bill is flowing out of the country** each year. That's the part of "national debt" that actually is a leak, as opposed to an internal transfer.

## But can our creditors tell us what to do?

This was the question lurking under all the others, and it comes from a real place. There's a famous, ugly version of creditor power: the **IMF "structural adjustment" programs** of the 1980s and 90s, where developing nations in crisis were lent money on the condition that they devalue their currency, privatise state assets, cut subsidies and slash spending ([IMF on conditionality](https://www.imf.org/en/about/factsheets/sheets/2023/imf-conditionality)). The lender effectively rewrote the borrower's budget. So how much of that could be done to us?

Almost none — and the reason is worth understanding. IMF conditionality only bites when a country takes an **IMF bailout loan**. NZ doesn't borrow from the IMF; our only relationship with it is the routine annual check-up that *every* member country gets ([the 2025 one](https://www.imf.org/en/news/articles/2025/05/23/pr25159-imf-executive-board-concludes-2025-article-iv-consultation-with-new-zealand) was a standard health-check, not a rescue). We raise money by selling our own bonds, and a bondholder's rights begin and end with the **bond's terms**: get paid the coupon and the principal on the dates promised. They have no contractual say over our tax rates or our spending. They aren't even a "they" — there's no single creditor to sit across a table and make demands, just thousands of dispersed investors.

What our creditors *can* do is leave. Lose their confidence and they'll demand higher yields, or stop buying; the rating agencies formalise the mood by [downgrading us](https://debtmanagement.treasury.govt.nz/investor-resources/credit-ratings), which lifts borrowing costs across the board. That's real pressure — but it's persuasion via price, not a contract that compels specific reforms. The government stays free to ignore it and pay more.

The deeper protection is the one the holders question hinted at: **we borrow in our own currency**. Even the ~60% of our bonds held offshore are denominated in NZD, not US dollars — and that matters enormously. A country forced to borrow in a foreign currency faces a trap: if its currency falls, the debt becomes literally unpayable, because it can't print dollars. That currency mismatch — "[original sin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Original_sin_(economics))" in the jargon — is what marches nations into the IMF's arms in the first place. NZ borrows in NZD, floats its exchange rate, and runs its own central bank, so it can always meet its nominal obligations; it can't be *forced* into default. (The honest caveat: the escape hatch is inflation or a falling dollar, which punishes bondholders and citizens in its own way. "Can't be forced to default" is not "no consequences.") You can even read this in our credit rating: S&P rates NZ's foreign-currency debt AA+ but its NZ-dollar debt a notch *higher* at AAA — the agencies themselves judge the debt we control the printing press for to be safer.

And the one time NZ did go through wrenching, IMF-style reform — the 1984 currency crisis and the "[Rogernomics](https://teara.govt.nz/en/economic-history/print)" overhaul that followed — we did it *to ourselves, by ourselves*. Floating the dollar, axing subsidies, selling off state enterprises: all domestic political choices, not conditions handed down from outside. Which is, in a bleak way, the real privilege the question is about — getting to inflict your structural adjustment on your own terms, rather than having it dictated as the price of a loan.

## The genuinely weird part: we borrow *and* we invest

Here's the thing that confused 2018-me the most. While the government is busy borrowing \$182 billion and paying interest on it, it *also* holds a large pile of shares. Back then I noticed about \$30 billion of them and demanded to know what they were invested in — *"it better not be oil, sugary food, alcohol, dairy, Facebook…"*

That pile is now about **\$59.6 billion** in Crown "share investments" ([Treasury FSG 2025](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-10/fsgnz-2025.pdf)), most of it sitting inside two funds: the **[NZ Super Fund](https://nzsuperfund.nz/) (~\$85bn)** and the **[ACC investment fund](https://www.acc.co.nz/) (~\$51bn)**.

Why borrow at interest while holding investments? Because the Super Fund is **pre-funding the pension**. The population is ageing; rather than dump the full cost of NZ Super on future taxpayers, the government saves and invests now so the burden is smoothed across generations — withdrawals don't even begin until the mid-2030s ([Guardians of NZ Super](https://nzsuperfund.nz/about-the-guardians/purpose-and-mandate/)). And the bet has paid off so far: over the 20 years to mid-2025 the Fund returned about **\$57 billion more than it would have cost the government to just hold that money as debt**. As long as the Fund's returns beat the government's borrowing rate over the long run — which equity-heavy portfolios are expected to — running the two side by side comes out ahead. (The honest counter-argument: that's an *expectation*, not a guarantee, and in a bad decade the money could have just paid down debt instead. Treasury's own [working paper on the Fund](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/wp/wp-21-01) is good on this.)

And the "better not be oil" worry? Partly reassuring, partly not. The Super Fund **does** formally exclude tobacco, cluster munitions, whale processing, civilian semi-automatic firearms, and a list of specific companies ([exclusions list](https://nzsuperfund.nz/how-we-invest/sustainable-finance/exclusions/)). But it does **not** blanket-exclude oil and gas — it runs a "reduce carbon and engage rather than divest" line instead. So: no tobacco, but some fossil fuels.

That tension is, remarkably, still live. In **April 2026 the High Court ruled the Fund's responsible-investment framework unlawful**, after a human-rights challenge, and ordered the Guardians to rebuild it around explicit standards they'd dropped in 2022 ([report](https://newswire.co.nz/2026/04/high-court-rules-nz-super-funds-ethical-investing-framework-unlawful-after-human-rights-challenge/)). The exact question I scrawled on a napkin in 2018 — *what are we, collectively, willing to own?* — is being argued in court right now.

## Should we just pay it off?

The instinct is yes: clear the debt, stop paying the interest, done. I shared that instinct — but the more I dug, the more I realised "zero debt" isn't a goal any country sensibly chases, and the reasons surprised me.

The mundane one is that debt is a **shock absorber**. With none of it, a pandemic or another Christchurch forces you to spike taxes or gut spending at the worst possible moment, instead of spreading the cost over years. And some borrowing is **positive-sum**: funding a hospital that will last 50 years with debt shares the cost with the future generations who'll actually use it, rather than billing today's taxpayers for the lot.

But the reason that genuinely surprised me is that **government bonds are infrastructure the financial system can't easily do without**. Government debt is the benchmark "risk-free" asset that everything else gets priced against. It's what banks hold to meet liquidity rules, what pension funds and insurers use to match decades-long promises, what the central bank buys and sells to run monetary policy, and the foundation of the market where everyone hedges interest-rate risk. Take it away and a surprising amount of financial plumbing has nothing left to stand on.

This isn't hypothetical. By the early 2000s Australia had run enough surpluses to have essentially no need to borrow — and it held a formal review on whether to simply shut its government bond market down. The decision, in 2003, was to **keep issuing bonds it didn't fiscally need**, precisely to preserve that benchmark yield curve, the interest-rate hedging market built on top of it, and the system's resilience in a crisis ([Australian Treasury, Review of the CGS Market](https://treasury.gov.au/review/cdmr/discussion-paper/chapter-3)). A government with no reason to borrow chose to borrow anyway, because the alternative was worse.

So the target isn't zero — it's **sustainable**: keeping debt growing no faster than the economy, so the *ratio* holds steady. At a stable debt-to-GDP you never actually have to pay it off; you roll the bonds over indefinitely and grow alongside them, the way a sensible company carries some debt rather than financing everything from cash. By that yardstick NZ is still **comparatively low** — the OECD average for general government debt is around 110% of GDP, and we're well under that on a comparable basis ([Budget 2025 fiscal context](https://www.budget.govt.nz/budget/2025/fiscal-strategy-report/fiscal-context.htm)).

The real worry isn't the level today — it's the **trajectory**. Net debt is forecast to keep climbing to a mid-40s percent of GDP before turning, and Treasury's long-term projections show it rising relentlessly toward 100%+ over the coming decades if nothing changes, driven mostly by the ageing population and health costs ([OECD NZ survey 2026](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/oecd-economic-surveys-new-zealand-2026_3ec5de98-en.html)). Which is, of course, exactly why the Super Fund exists.

## What I still don't know

True to the spirit of this blog, answering the questions mostly produced better ones:

- If ~60% of the interest leaves the country, how much should we care about the *foreign* share specifically versus the total? Is "owing it to ourselves" really so different?
- The Super Fund beat the cost of debt over 20 good years. How confident should we be it keeps doing so — and who eats the loss if it doesn't?
- Treasury runs decades-long forecasts to justify all of this. How accurate have those forecasts actually been? I never did check.

Still more questions than answers. But at least now I know roughly where the \$1,670 goes.

[^measures]: One caveat for the pedantic: Treasury re-based the "net core Crown debt" measure around 2022, so percentage-of-GDP comparisons that straddle that year (like 2019 vs 2025) aren't perfectly like-for-like. The direction of travel — sharply up — is not in doubt.
