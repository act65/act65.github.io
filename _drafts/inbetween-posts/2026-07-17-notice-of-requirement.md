## Premise

The Concordat — the galactic legal order — once expanded by unannounced harvest of living worlds. After an era of atrocity and scandal, its great humanitarian jurist authored the reform that is celebrated as the moment law entered the void: **the Requirement**. No world may be incorporated (annexed, harvested, its populations indentured) until formal Notice has been served upon it.

Notice is served in the canonical legal modality: inscription upon **the Register**, a substrate written in modulated gravitational pulses, which every Concordat species perceives natively — indeed, *perceiving the Register is the legal test of personhood*. A statutory period of silence after service constitutes consent. Resistance after service is unlawful, and forfeits the resisters' autonomy.

The gerrymander at the heart of it: a pre-spacefaring species **cannot perceive the Register, by definition** — and its inability to answer is what makes its incorporation lawful. The reform did not end the harvests. It notarized them.

(Historical irony to preserve: like Palacios Rubios's Requerimiento, the Requirement really was written by the *moderates*. It was the humanitarian fix.)

## POV

**A Notary Third-Class** — the certifier. Their job: accompany service vessels, witness the Reading, affix the seal, file the certificate. The Oviedo figure: the functionary who carries the document and slowly understands what his paperwork is for.

Worldbuilding grace note: the notary's species uses names that are performative legal formulas — the notary's name translates to something like *Sworn-Before-Witnesses*. Their entire culture is oath-shaped; the Register's integrity is their civilization's founding sacrament. This makes the ending cost something.

**Formal constraint (important):** humanity appears *only* through its radio archive — transcripts, recordings, telemetry the notary reviews. We never see a human on the page. Humanity is heard but never seen; the reader occupies the notary's epistemic position exactly.

## Beats

1. **Career montage.** Routine servings: dead worlds; microbial worlds; a world of methane drifters certified "no cognizable response" — though the drifters sang, in pressure-waves, for six local years afterward. Noise, legally. The notary keeps a private habit: retaining samples of the noise. First crack.
2. **The Reading at Sol.** The service vessel arrives; the Requirement is inscribed — gravitational modulation washing through the system. On Earth, detectors (LIGO's descendants) record a structured signal. Humanity *knows within days* that it is artificial. This is the key divergence from 1513 and the source of the story's particular horror: the natives on the beach can partially read the document — and still cannot answer it. Answering requires modulating gravity: mass-drivers, collapsed matter. Humanity has radio.
3. **The screaming file.** Over the statutory period, humanity replies with everything it has: mathematics in radio, laser pulses, the whole Arecibo playbook, a crewed probe carrying gifts and a child's voice counting primes. None of it is cognizable. The file records: *"No response inscribed. Ambient electromagnetic noise noted and archived, per procedure."* The notary, against procedure, listens to the noise. All of it. There is music in it.
4. **The courtesy translation.** The notary commits a sanctionable irregularity: beams a radio translation of the Requirement — legally void, an act of pity or of conscience, they cannot tell which. Now humanity understands *fully*: the deadline, the terms, the forfeiture. Cut to transcripts: panic; then something better than panic — Earth's lawyers, working from the decoded corpus of Concordat law, draft a formal **objection** in the Concordat's own legal idiom, arguing service was defective under the Concordat's own principles. It is a *good argument*. Broadcast in radio. Which means, legally, it does not exist. It is a brief filed into a register the court cannot hear, by a party that does not exist, in response to a notice it could not read. (Modern echo the afterword will draw out: service by publication — notice printed in a newspaper the defendant will never see, deemed legally sufficient.)
5. **The inscription.** The climax is a clerk's decision. The notary can do the one thing no one may do: forge an entry — inscribe humanity's objection onto the Register under a false seal. Forgery of the Register is the Concordat's unforgivable crime; for the notary's oath-shaped culture it is self-desecration. They do it.
6. **The trial (the trick of the ending).** The entry is discovered and annulled — but annulment requires a hearing, and the hearing requires the forged content to be read into evidence. **Humanity's objection becomes legally audible for the first and only time as Exhibit A in the trial of its forger.** The court hears it, in full, in the canonical modality. It rules the original service proper. Incorporation proceeds. The notary is unmade — name revoked, the worst of their culture's punishments. But exhibits are permanent. The objection is in the record forever, filed under the notary's crime.
7. **Epilogue — a study guide.** (The Wai-terra move: end on a bureaucratic document.) Centuries later, Concordat law students study *the Terran Annex* — the classic case that eventually forced the modality reform: notice must now be served in a modality demonstrably perceptible to the recipient. The reform is named after the great jurist, long dead. Not after the notary. Not after Earth, whose incorporation was, the study guide notes, procedurally sound. Final line, from the exam: *"Discuss: to whom was the Requirement addressed?"*

## Themes

- Consent theater: law performed *at* someone, *for* the benefit of the performer's own courts and conscience.
- Personhood tests as gerrymander — the criterion for having a voice is designed to be unmeetable.
- Reform that launders: the humanitarian fix that makes atrocity procedurally sound.
- The record vs. reality; the certificate as the crime's true product.
- The clerk as moral pivot (Oviedo, Morel): the person whose job is the paperwork is the first to see what the paperwork is for.

## For the afterword (real history to carry)

- 1511: Montesinos's sermon on Hispaniola ("Are these not men?") scandalizes the Crown → junta of Burgos → Laws of Burgos and the Requerimiento (1513), drafted by the jurist Palacios Rubios — the humanitarian reform of its day.
- The text: world history from Adam, the papal donation of 1493, the demand for submission; "the deaths and losses which shall accrue from this are your fault, not ours."
- The practice: read in Spanish or Latin, no interpreter, to empty villages, from shipboard, at night before raids; notarized — the certificate was the legal product.
- Oviedo (who carried it in Panama): should we keep it "until we have some Indian in a cage" who can understand it?
- Las Casas: on reading it, he did not know whether to laugh or weep.
- Vitoria's lectures demolish the papal-donation title (and seed international law); 1556: the word "conquest" is officially replaced with "pacification."
- Modern analogues: service by publication; constructive notice; clickwrap / terms-of-service "consent"; (comic ancestor: the planning notice "on display at Alpha Centauri" in Hitchhiker's Guide).

## Open questions

- Is Earth named early, or revealed? (Lean: never named — identified only by details in the noise archive; the reader recognizes home the way the notary can't.)
- Interleave excerpts from the great jurist's celebrated memoir ("I gave the silent worlds a voice in law") against the practice? Cheap irony risk vs. strong counterpoint.
- How much of humanity's objection do we actually get on the page at the trial? (Maybe all of it — the story's only long human speech, delivered by the enemy's court reader.)
